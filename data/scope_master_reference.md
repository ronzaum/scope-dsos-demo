## What Scope AI Does

Scope AI builds AI-powered inspection automation for the Testing, Inspection, and Certification (TIC) industry. Over a million inspectors globally still use pen, paper, Excel, and Word to assess infrastructure, factories, and goods. Scope replaces that manual reporting layer with AI that writes error-free test, audit, and inspection reports.

**How it works:**
- Writes error-free test, audit, and inspection reports automatically
- Normalises formatting across report types and client types
- Finds and compares audit results against historical data
- Checks reports against regulation and client standards automatically
- Extracts data from technical documents (PDFs, Excel, manuals, diagrams)
- Populates enterprise systems (CMMS, EAM)
- Generates inspection schemas and Written Schemes of Examination (WSEs)
- Anomaly detection: flags historical errors, reduces risk of major incidents

**Performance:** 45% reduction in inspection cycle times, average reporting time down to 12 minutes, 2.2x reduction in end-to-end inspection time.

**Revenue model:** SaaS. Bespoke AI systems per client, deeply embedded into customer workflows. Customers are TIC companies (the firms that inspect), not asset owners directly.

**Moat:** Bespoke per client. Each deployment requires a custom data layer mapping that client's specific inspection workflows, document types, and regulatory standards. This makes switching costly once embedded. Only AI-native inspection platform custom-built for TIC. Not a general inspection SaaS with AI bolted on.

**Clients:** 7 of the top 10 TIC companies globally. End customers include Shell, AstraZeneca, SpaceX. Customer base represents 21% of UK GDP.

**Location:** Clerkenwell, London. On-site.

## The Deployment Strategist Role

### What It Actually Is

The title is "Deployment Strategist" but this is a **growth + implementation hybrid**. Three real functions:

**1. Deployment (primary):** Translate complex operational challenges into concrete AI deployments. Take enterprise clients from signed contract to working product. Understand their inspection workflows, map Scope's product to their reality, drive adoption from zero.

**2. Account expansion (commercial):** Grow the value of accounts through delivery, not pipeline. Commission on new account growth signals this is not a pure delivery role.

**3. Product intelligence (secondary):** Feed learning back into the product. Become an expert in applying AI to solve real-world problems. A forward-deployed observer as much as an executor.

The sequence: deeply understand client needs → strategise the right AI solution → hands-on to secure implementation. Drive growth and scale accounts through delivery, not through pipeline.

### What the Role Demands

1. **Structured thinker who executes** — break complex client situations into deployment plans and drive to completion
2. **Commercial instinct** — understand that deployment is only valuable if it expands into growth; spot expansion signals
3. **High EQ for enterprise relationships** — build trust with senior industrial stakeholders who are sceptical of AI and change
4. **Fast learner on domain** — TIC is new to most people; pick it up quickly and run
5. **"Co-builder" mentality** — not an execution drone; bring ideas and feedback into the product loop

### Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | Description |
|---|---|
| Over-promises to clients | Creates expectations Scope can't meet; trust collapses |
| Delivery without expansion | Completes deployments but doesn't grow account value |
| Domain paralysis | Gets stuck on TIC specifics instead of reading the underlying constraint |
| Slow learner | Can't become fluent enough in each client's world fast enough |
| Stakeholder avoidance | Comfortable with junior users but avoids senior or resistant stakeholders |
| Loose structure | Can't produce the "hyper-structured deployment plans" the JD explicitly calls for |

### Responsibility Breakdown

**Strategic Planning & Execution:**
Design and implement hyper-structured deployment plans. Start by deeply understanding the customer: how they work, what language they use, what tools they have today, what their biggest pain point is. Then map: what is the immediate solve, what is their long-term goal, how fast can we implement, which feature or user type within the enterprise is most relevant right now.

**Creative Problem Solving:**
Navigate unforeseen roadblocks. Problems are only solvable if properly captured and classified first. Turn reactive firefighting into structured resolution. The system surfaces the right context; the DS makes the call.

**Client Engagement:**
Build trust by understanding the client's emotional reality, not just their operational one. The DS is the person the client trusts to understand their world. That trust is the product's moat at the relationship level.

**Retention & Intelligence Compounding:**
Build a database of learnings and applied rules that compounds over time. Everything learned feeds back. Every deployment makes the next one better.

## The Core Tension: Bespoke vs Scale

Scope's moat (bespoke data layers per client) is also the scaling constraint. Each client requires custom workflow mapping, and that does not scale linearly with headcount.

**The bottleneck:** Scope can win deals. They need to deliver bespoke implementations faster without proportionally growing headcount. The company needs to systematise deployment without losing the depth that wins deals.

**Two paths (not mutually exclusive):**

**Path A — Segment and Concentrate:** Choose specific client segments where inspection workflows are similar. Build a repeatable playbook for that segment first. Gain trust and case studies within one segment, then expand. Advantage: faster time-to-repeatable. Risk: narrows the funnel during a growth phase.

**Path B — Extract the Common Layer:** Study all existing and past client deployments. Map the underlying inspection logic across customers. Identify the repeatable elements (document types, regulatory check patterns, reporting structures) and the truly bespoke elements (client-specific data schemas, integration points). Build a deployment system with repeatable steps that work across any client, with defined branch points where customisation enters.

**What works:** A repeatable workflow of structured, stepwise automation. Each step is a checkpoint. The repeatable parts run systematically; the bespoke parts are flagged for DS judgment. The pipeline is the same logic applied to every client, with customisation contained within specific steps rather than spread across the entire process.

## TIC Client Types

### By Scale

**Enterprise (top 10 TIC, 1000+ inspectors):**
Bureau Veritas, SGS, Intertek, TÜV SÜD, Eurofins, DNV. Typical contract: £150k-£500k+ annual. Sales cycle: 3-6 months. Need phased rollout or hybrid deployment. Key success factor: executive sponsor who can protect budget through adoption ramp. Key risk: internal politics, multiple divisions with competing priorities. Expansion pattern: win one division, prove ROI, expand to adjacent divisions.

**Mid-Market (regional TIC, 100-1000 inspectors):**
Regional inspection firms, specialist sector companies. Typical contract: £50k-£150k annual. Sales cycle: 1-3 months. Remote or hybrid deployment. Key success factor: speed to value. Mid-market won't wait 3 months for Phase 1. Key risk: budget constraints, single decision-maker dependency.

### By Inspection Type

**Protocol-Driven (ISO, regulatory-mandated sequences):**
Sectors: automotive, aerospace, nuclear, pharma, food safety. Inspectors follow exact sequences on autopilot, 4-6 inspections daily. Any deviation from their flow is slower than paper. Deployment rule: on-site observation BEFORE template configuration. NEVER configure from documentation alone. Adoption driver: tool must be faster than paper for their specific sequence. Adoption killer: extra steps, wrong sequence, incomplete localisation.

**Flexible (inspector discretion, varied formats):**
Sectors: construction, consumer products, general industrial. Inspectors have more discretion in how they conduct inspections. Willing to adapt workflow. Remote configuration can work if mobile UI is clean. Adoption driver: mobile convenience, photo-to-report automation, time savings. Adoption killer: slow mobile UI, too many taps, poor offline support.

### By Geography

**UK:** English language default. Strong TIC presence (BV, Intertek, TÜV SÜD all have UK operations). Regulatory: UKAS, HSE, Building Safety Act.

**DACH (Germany, Austria, Switzerland):** German language MANDATORY. 100% localisation before deployment. TÜV brands dominate. High standards, high protocol adherence. Regulatory: KBA (automotive), TÜV standards, DIN. Cultural note: thoroughness valued over speed.

**Global (multi-country):** Multi-language support critical. Time zone considerations. Local regulatory formats per country. Phased by geography: prove in one country, replicate.

### User Types Within TIC Clients

**Field Inspector:** On-site 6-8 hours, needs mobile capture, 4-6 inspections daily. Optimises for speed. Will revert to paper if digital is slower. Key: mobile UI must be frictionless.

**Supervisor:** Reviews 5-8 reports daily, flags non-compliance. Needs summary views, not raw data. If supervisors adopt, they pull inspectors toward adoption organically.

**Compliance Lead:** Monthly regulatory reporting, audit prep, trend analysis. Needs output in exact regulatory format (KBA, UKAS, etc). Will not engage until report format matches their regulatory requirements.

**Report Consumer:** Internal or external client receiving the inspection output. Needs clean, standardised reports.

## Strategic Reads

- **Immediate priority:** Scale deployment capacity to match revenue growth. They can win deals but need to deliver bespoke implementations faster
- **Long-term ambition:** Become the infrastructure layer for TIC globally. "Freeing the physical world to rapidly build"
- **Core tension:** Bespoke-per-client model is the moat but also the scaling bottleneck. Each new client requires deep workflow mapping, custom data layers, and domain immersion. The company needs to systematise this without losing the depth that wins deals
- **What is underdeveloped:** Deployment playbooks, repeatable onboarding systems, GTM infrastructure. The bespoke model works but does not scale linearly with headcount

## Positioning Angles

**Angles that resonate:**
1. "Build deployment systems, not just deploy." Scope's bottleneck is converting bespoke client work into repeatable playbooks. Anyone who can systematise onboarding and create a scalable deployment engine is solving the exact constraint on growth
2. "Done this in a different regulated industry." AEC to TIC is a genuine transfer: regulated environments, physical infrastructure, compliance documentation, stakeholder complexity. The mechanism is the same
3. "Embed with clients and build trust with senior stakeholders." The DS role is client-facing in complex enterprise environments. Proving comfort with ambiguity, senior stakeholder management, and structured problem-solving in unfamiliar domains is critical

**Language that lands:**
- "Deployment architecture" / "onboarding systems" / "client data layer"
- "Regulated environments" / "compliance-driven workflows"
- "Bespoke to repeatable" / "systematised without losing depth"
- "Structured problem solving" / "co-builder"
- "Hyper-structured" (their own word)

**What to avoid:**
- Do not lead with AI/tech credentials — they want an operator, not a technologist
- Do not use generic SaaS deployment language — they operate in a physical, regulated, document-heavy world
- Do not underestimate the bespoke complexity — each client is different. Cookie-cutter language will signal a lack of understanding
